Edge-Adjusted Performance Metrics for
Intraday Options Strategy Evaluation

An Empirical Framework Using R-Multiples and Breakeven Win Rates

Working Paper — April 2026

Dataset: 4.09M trades • 740 configurations • 267 trading days • SPX 0DTE options

Abstract. We present an empirical framework for evaluating intraday options trading strategies that replaces composite scoring with statistically grounded edge metrics. Using a dataset of 4.09 million simulated trades across 740 strategy configurations and 267 trading days (March 2025–April 2026) on 0DTE SPX options, we demonstrate that conventional performance measures — including win rate, Sharpe ratio, and weighted composite scores — systematically misidentify losing strategies as top performers. We introduce an edge-based ranking system built on three metrics: the R-multiple (average win percentage divided by average loss percentage), the breakeven win rate implied by a strategy’s payoff asymmetry, and the edge (observed win rate minus breakeven win rate). We show that 38% of strategies with win rates exceeding 70% carry negative edge and lose money, while strategies with 59% win rates but R-multiples above 2.8 generate the highest risk-adjusted returns. Walk-forward analysis across four independent 67-day windows demonstrates coefficient of variation below 0.10 for top strategies. All results survive Bonferroni correction for 740 simultaneous hypothesis tests at the 0.05 family-wise significance level.
Keywords: options trading, strategy evaluation, R-multiple, edge metrics, 0DTE options, walk-forward analysis, multiple testing correction, payoff asymmetry

1 Dataset Overview

The dataset spans 267 consecutive trading days from March 27, 2025 through April 20, 2026. All trades are on SPX Weekly (SPXW) 0DTE options. Strategy configurations share a common signal architecture — Hull Moving Average (HMA) crossover detection on option contract price bars — and vary across HMA periods, timeframes, take-profit/stop-loss levels, strike offsets, and execution modes.

All trade returns are net of friction: half-spread of max($0.05, price × 1%) per side, $0.35 commission per contract per side, plus order-type-specific slippage ($0.002/contract on market and stop orders). A participation-rate cap of 20% prevents backtested quantities from exceeding printed bar volume.

2 The Edge Framework

Win rate alone is meaningless without reference to payoff structure. A strategy that wins 75% of trades while gaining +10% per win and losing -50% per loss is a losing strategy. The edge framework makes this explicit.

2.1 Definitions

R-Multiple = Avg Win % ÷ |Avg Loss %| How large wins are relative to losses. R > 1 means wins are bigger than losses.
Breakeven Win Rate = |Avg Loss| ÷ (Avg Win + |Avg Loss|) = 1 ÷ (1 + R) The minimum win rate needed to break even, given the payoff ratio.
Edge = Observed Win Rate − Breakeven Win Rate Positive = profitable strategy. Negative = losing strategy regardless of win rate.
Expected Value = (WR × Avg Win %) + ((1 − WR) × Avg Loss %) The expected percentage return per trade.

2.2 Why This Matters

Central Finding: 38% of strategies with win rates above 70% carry negative edge and lose money. Win rate is not predictive of profitability without the payoff ratio.

7 Statistical Significance

With 740 simultaneous hypothesis tests, we apply Bonferroni correction to control the family-wise error rate at α = 0.05. This requires z > 4.08 for any individual strategy to be declared significant.

Configuration Win Rate BE WR Edge n (trades) z-score Required z Significant?
All top positive-edge configurations survive Bonferroni correction by an order of magnitude (z-scores of 49–118 vs. threshold of 4.08). The negative-edge control is also significantly negative (z = -14.5), confirming the framework detects both real edge and real anti-edge.

10 Discussion & Limitations

10.1 Why Composite Scores Fail

The composite score previously used was: Score = (WR × 40) + (clamp(Sharpe,0,1) × 30) + (AvgPnl>0 ? 20 : 0) + (WorstDay>-500 ? 10 : 0). This formula weights win rate at 40% without reference to payoff structure, clamps Sharpe at 1.0 (non-discriminating for high-frequency strategies), and uses binary P&L thresholds with cliff effects. The edge framework replaces all four components with a single metric that directly answers: "Does this strategy win often enough, given its payoff ratio, to make money?"

10.2 Limitations

In-sample optimization. All 740 configurations were designed with knowledge of the full 267-day sample. A true out-of-sample test requires a holdout period not seen during strategy development. The walk-forward quarterly analysis partially mitigates this concern but does not fully resolve it.

Non-independence of trades. The z-test assumes independent observations. Intraday trades on the same underlying share market microstructure. The effective sample size may be smaller than the nominal trade count. Clustering standard errors by trading day would provide more conservative inference.

Single underlying. All results are on SPX 0DTE options. Generalizability to other underlyings remains untested.

Fill model assumptions. While more realistic than unconstrained backtesting (participation-rate cap, price-proportional spreads, order-type slippage), the model still assumes fills at bar-level prices rather than tick-level order book simulation.

10.3 Practitioner Implications

  1. Never evaluate by win rate alone. Always compute the breakeven win rate. A "70% WR" may need 83% to break even.
  2. R-multiples above 2.0 create safety margin. With R=2, breakeven is 33%. Even significant signal degradation from 60% to 45% preserves profitability.
  3. Fixed sizing isolates signal quality. Edge is computed in percentage terms, not dollars. Size optimization is a separate problem to solve only after edge is confirmed.
